### Why reading fiction still matters

Another point worth making is that fiction encourages empathy. ### A different way to learn

When Marta signed up for a community theatre project, she did not expect it to help her English. She had joined because a friend persuaded her to try something new after work. At first, she was mainly worried about pronunciation. Reading lines aloud in front of other people made her uncomfortable, and she assumed that everyone else in the group was naturally more confident than she was.

What changed her mind was the atmosphere of the rehearsals. Nobody laughed when someone made a mistake, and the director paid as much attention to clarity and expression as to technical accuracy. Marta began to notice that successful communication depended on more than perfect grammar. Timing, tone of voice and eye contact all affected whether an audience understood the meaning behind a line.

Over the next few months, she also became a better listener. During rehearsals, missing a single phrase could make it difficult to respond properly in the next scene. This forced her to concentrate hard on what the other actors were saying rather than on the sentence she planned to say next. That habit gradually carried over into real conversations.

Marta still studies in conventional ways too. She uses vocabulary notebooks, works through exam tasks and reviews feedback on her writing. However, she now believes that learning improves fastest when language is connected to action, feeling and interaction. For her, theatre was valuable not because it replaced serious study, but because it showed her what serious study was actually for.

### Studying with other people

Some learners avoid group study because they assume it wastes time. They imagine long conversations, poor concentration and too many opportunities to become distracted. In some cases, they are right. A badly organised study session can easily turn into a social event with very little learning value. __(27)__

One reason is that explaining an idea to someone else forces you to check whether you truly understand it. A grammar point that seems clear in your head may suddenly feel less certain when you have to put it into words. __(28)__

Group study can also help motivation. Working alone requires a lot of self-discipline, especially after a tiring day. If you know that someone else is expecting you to join a short session, you are often more likely to make the effort. __(29)__

Write the correct letter in each box. You may use any letter more than once.

32. the importance of reviewing progress in a visual way
33. benefiting from combining movement with study
34. changing study methods according to energy levels
35. realising that short sessions can still be effective
36. using competition as a source of motivation

### A

I used to believe that serious study only counted if I was sitting at a desk with all my notes open. That idea sounded disciplined, but in practice it often meant I postponed work until I had a large block of free time. Eventually I started using ten-minute revision sessions on my phone while travelling, and I was surprised by how much they added up over a week.

### B

For a while I copied the study routine of a friend who liked waking up very early. I tried it for two weeks and felt terrible. Now I accept that my concentration changes during the day. If I feel mentally fresh, I do reading tasks or writing. If I am tired, I switch to vocabulary review or listening rather than forcing myself through something heavy.

### C

I keep a large wall planner above my desk and mark every study session on it. It is simple, but it helps more than any app I have tried. When I see several empty days, I react immediately. When I see a solid pattern, I feel calmer because the progress is visible. It reminds me that consistency matters more than mood.

### D

I learn best when my body is active. If I sit still for too long, I lose focus, so I often walk while reviewing speaking phrases or recording answers on my phone. I also enjoy timed challenges with classmates. Trying to finish a task slightly faster than someone else makes me concentrate harder than when I work completely alone.
